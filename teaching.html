Teaching Statement

Collaborative Learning

I fell in love with mathematics at my high school’s calculus camp. At calculus camp, students were placed in study groups to work on calculus problems all day. This didn’t sound very fun at first, but I quickly lost track of time as I worked on math problems with my peers. In this kind of environment, I learned the excitement of discussing mathematics with others. My favorite part of discussing math is figuring out how to communicate different approaches to a single problem, and reaching a collective feeling of accomplishment once everyone understands a concept. Since high school, my dream has been to teach mathematics.

Teaching Experience

At Duke University, I was a teaching assistant for Math 111L: Laboratory Calculus I during Fall 2017, an instructor of record for Math 105L: Laboratory Calculus and Functions I during Fall 2018 and Fall 2019, and an instructor of record for Math 106L: Laboratory Calculus and Functions II during Spring 2020. In all my courses, I strive to create content that is interesting and accessible, yet challenging. My advice to math students is to practice talking about mathematics —to continuously discuss specific problems or general concepts with classmates and instructors.

Perspective on Learning

I encourage students to avoid rote application of formulas and techniques, and instead focus on approaching the material conceptually. I want my students to learn to be comfortable with not knowing answers immediately, and to gain confidence in their knowledge and skillset to try new ideas. In class, my students will explore one theorem in multiple ways —algebraically, geometrically, numerically, etc —and practice how to compare and choose. Not only does this lead to a deeper understanding of the material, but my students learn flexible problem solving skills which can be used across all disciplines.

However, I recognize that this is much easier said than done, especially in the presence of pre-existing barriers to mathematics. For example, 105L and 106L are often the first math classes a student will take at Duke, and many high schools, both U.S. and international, focus on standardized testing over conceptual learning. Not only do standardized tests prevent students from engaging with the material in a productive way, but they have also proven to be biased against women, gender minorities, and BIPOC (Moss, 1989), and are poor indicators of success (Kartal, 2016).

Some of my students struggled with (or have not taken) the AP Calculus exam and initially lacked confidence in mathematics. However, after covering foundational mathematical concepts without any formulas or memorization, they learned how to build more complex ideas from the ground up. For instance, in Math 106L, we spent more than a week practicing how to approximate the area under a curve using Riemann sums, simply by adding up the areas of rectangles that roughly fill the area. Then, as a class we discussed how to improve our estimate by using more rectangles, each smaller in width. Finally, we establised the exact area under a curve —the definite integral —as the Riemann sum of n rectangles, with n approaching infinity.

Teaching Values

I try to adapt my teaching style to the composition of students in a particular semester. My 105L class during Fall 2018 had four students, which allowed for individualized attention. I had my students up at the chalkboard with me regularly, in place of timed quizzes. In Fall 2019, my 105L class had 17 students, a perfect size for doing in-class activities in groups of three or four. During lectures, I had students chime in or ask questions, and I regularly had students volunteer to come up to the board to explain a problem. To give students more opportunities for individualized attention in my larger classes, I arrive to class 30 minutes early and leave 30 minutes late each day, hold extra office hours, and check in regularly with students who have struggled with the material.

Additionally, I take student feedback very seriously. I rely a lot on my interactions with students in class and during office hours, as well as on written feedback. I pay attention to the reactions of my students during class, ask lots of questions, and slow down when needed. I am always adapting to what works. My favorite student feedback, from office hours, was one asking for more graphs and pictures. Even for calculus concepts often discussed algebraically, a few extra graphs on the board helped some students to grasp the concepts better, and others to understand a new perspective and gain a deeper understanding of the material.

Diversity and Inclusion

Finally, I am a strong proponent of diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) work in mathematics. In Spring 2019, I founded the Duke chapter of the Association for Women in Mathematics (AWM), a club dedicated to creating an inclusive and tight-knit community of mathematicians at Duke, and to promoting interest in mathematics. Besides through AWM, I actively support all departmental DEI initiatives. Recently, the Duke mathematics department has been discussing antiracist plans in response to a petition written on August 3rd, 2020. I believe that my impact as an educator extends beyond the classroom, and my goal as a math teacher is to not only teach well, but to help create support and community for my students and colleagues.
